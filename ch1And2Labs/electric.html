Electric Power History

Brief History of Electric Power

Thomas Edison’s first electric power plant generated 110-volt direct current (DC) for lighting and variable-speed machinery in nearby buildings. However, efficient transmission of power over long distances requires higher voltages. At the time, the only way to transform DC voltage was to use a “genset,” which consisted of a DC motor driving a DC generator. This setup required two rotating machines and four windings, making it complex and inefficient.

Nikola Tesla later invented the alternating current (AC) induction motor and transformer. With a constant AC frequency, an induction motor’s speed cannot vary, but it is cheaper and more durable than a DC motor. Additionally, a transformer has no moving parts and only two windings, making it more efficient and reliable than a DC genset. As a result, AC power won the “war of the currents” at the dawn of the 20th century.

This dominance did not last forever. In the middle of the 20th century, computers began consuming increasing amounts of DC power. The invention of the insulated-gate bipolar transistor (IGBT) and the voltage-source converter (VSC) made it possible to efficiently convert and control electrical power. A VSC can generate variable-frequency AC, allowing induction motors to run at variable speeds, and it can easily transform DC power.

Today, DC power, computer control, and the internet are helping the power grid integrate variable renewable resources such as wind and solar energy. These technologies are also improving electrical distribution systems on campuses, in neighborhoods, and within buildings. With the addition of local energy generation and storage, distribution systems are evolving into robust systems known as microgrids.

Key Milestones

  • 1882: Edison’s Pearl Street DC power plant in New York
  • 1884: Tesla invents the closed-core AC transformer in Budapest
  • 1886: Thorenberg AC power plant in Lucerne
  • 1985: Insulated-gate bipolar transistor developed by Toshiba
  • 2012: UCSD 42-megawatt microgrid in southern California
