Brief History of Electric Power

Thomas Edison’s first electric power plant generated 110-volt direct current for lighting and variable-speed machinery in nearby buildings. The system worked well for small areas but could not easily transmit power over long distances.

Nikola Tesla later invented the alternating current induction motor and transformer. With a constant AC frequency, transformers made it possible to transmit power at high voltage and low current, then step it down for safe local use.

This breakthrough allowed electric power systems to expand rapidly across cities and regions. Large centralized power plants became practical, and electricity became a common utility.

Today, modern power systems combine traditional generation with renewable resources such as wind and solar, along with advanced control systems and power electronics.

  • 1882: Edison’s Pearl Street power plant in New York
  • 1884: Tesla invents the AC transformer
  • 1886: Thorenberg AC power plant in Lucerne
  • 1985: IGBT developed by Toshiba
  • 2012: UCSD 42-megawatt microgrid
