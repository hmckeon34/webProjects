Lawrence Hydropower

Part A: History of Lawrence Hydropower

In 1874, residents of Lawrence, Kansas had burned all of the region’s available wood, and fossil fuels were not yet accessible. To address this problem, Orlando Darling built a dam across the Kansas River north of town and ran mechanical cables from a water wheel to power local industries.

Justin Bowersock later purchased the operation and named it The Bowersock Mills & Power Company (Bowersock). The company’s slogan reflects its long-standing mission:

Producing clean, renewable hydropower since 1874

Part B: Expansion and Modern Operation

By 1885, twelve water wheels powered two flouring mills, a paper mill, two elevators, a twine factory, a shirt factory, two machine shops, the Leis Chemical Works, two printing offices, the barbed wire works, and several other smaller industries. After rebuilding from severe ice damage in 1888, Bowersock introduced four dynamos that converted raw mechanical power into electrical energy.

To supplement the original 2.4 megawatt plant on the south side of the river, Bowersock later constructed a new 4.6 megawatt plant on the north side. To finance this expansion, Bowersock signed a 25-year power-purchase agreement with Kansas City’s Board of Public Utilities (BPU), requiring that:

Bowersock must send BPU an annual average power output greater than or equal to 80% of its total generating capacity.

Instantaneous power generation is proportional to the water flow rate multiplied by the difference in water height. Because river flow varies with rainfall, the amount of power delivered also fluctuates. To maximize deliverable energy, Bowersock employs a run-of-the-river policy, maintaining the upstream water level as high as possible while using whatever flow is available.

Energy is stored in the height of the upstream water behind the dam. By intentionally varying this height, Bowersock could theoretically use stored energy to offset changes in local power consumption or renewable generation. However, doing so could violate its contractual obligations.

Until the BPU contract ends, energy stored in the water behind the dam is not available to Lawrence.
